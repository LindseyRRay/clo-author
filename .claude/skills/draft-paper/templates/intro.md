# Introduction Template

**Target length:** 1,000–1,500 words (~3–4 pages).
**Position in paper:** First section after abstract.
**Reads first:** This is what most readers see before deciding whether to continue.

## Required elements (must all appear)

- Phenomenon hook with concrete grounding (no jargon in first paragraph)
- Research question stated in plain English
- Contribution statement within the first 2 pages
- Identification strategy (1 paragraph)
- Enumerated findings, each with magnitude and units
- Limitations paragraph (honest, not defensive)
- Literature review (thematic, integrated into prose)
- Closing contribution sentence

## Paragraph-by-paragraph scaffold

### Paragraph 1 — Broad context (~100–150 words)
- Open with the phenomenon or puzzle in accessible, engaging language a layperson would find interesting.
- Ground it with a concrete example, statistic, or anecdote.
- No citations yet.
- No paper-specific machinery.
- Closing sentence should pose a tension or question that the rest of the intro will engage.

### Paragraph 2 — Sharpen the tension (~100–150 words)
- Introduce the tension that motivates the paper. NOT a "gap in the literature" framing.
- Example structures: "Although X, Y remains unclear." / "While prior work has shown A, the implications for B are not understood." / "Two views compete: …"
- May begin citations here.
- Last sentence: previews the question the paper will answer.

### Paragraph 3 — Research question + what we do + main finding (~150–200 words)
- One sentence: the research question, phrased crisply.
- One-to-two sentences: "We study [setting] using [data]. We exploit [source of variation] for identification."
- One-to-three sentences: "We find …" with magnitude and units. Lead with the headline number.
- Last sentence: "These findings imply…" — preview the so-what.

### Paragraph 4 — Setting and data (~150 words)
- Concrete description of empirical context. Why this setting? Why this data?
- Sample size, time range, key unit of observation.
- Closing sentence: bridges to identification.

### Paragraph 5 — Identification strategy (~150–200 words)
- Source of variation, in plain English.
- Why it works for the question — chain logical necessity (writing-style rule 4).
- Key threat to identification + how the design addresses it.
- Closing: "This design allows us to identify [estimand]."

### Paragraphs 6–N — Enumerated findings (~150–200 words each, 1 per finding block)
- "I have three sets of findings."  (Use "we" if co-authored.)
- Each finding paragraph:
  - Topic sentence stating what the finding is, with magnitude.
  - Evidence sentence (the spec or the figure that shows it).
  - Robustness sentence ("This result is robust to …" — one-liner).
  - Interpretation sentence (what it means, separate from what it is — writing-style rule 13).

### Paragraph N+1 — Limitations (~100 words)
- Direct, not defensive.
- 2–3 honest limitations.
- One sentence on what data cannot show.

### Paragraph N+2 — Literature review (~200–300 words)
- Thematically organized, NOT chronological.
- Use comparatives: "Consistent with X, we find…" / "By contrast, Y find …".
- Integrate citations into prose, never as standalone lists.
- 2–4 sub-themes max.

### Closing paragraph — Contribution + roadmap (~100 words)
- One sentence summarizing what the paper contributes that is new.
- Brief roadmap: "Section 2 describes the setting. Section 3 … Section 6 concludes." (Optional — drop if implicit.)

## Banned patterns (recheck before submission)

- "This paper contributes to the literature on X." → instead show the contribution through findings.
- "There is a gap in the literature on Y." → instead state the tension that exists.
- "Interestingly", "remarkably", "it is worth noting", "arguably". 
- Any "significant effect" without a number.
- Mixing results and interpretation in the same sentence.
- Citation dumps (5+ parenthetical citations without prose).

## Voice
- First person singular for sole-authored. First person plural for co-authored. Be consistent.
- Active voice by default.
- Short sentences mixed with medium ones.

## Length norms from example papers
- Generative AI at Work: intro ~1,400 words, 11 paragraphs.
- Market Effects of Algorithms: intro ~1,800 words, 14 paragraphs (longer — multi-finding paper).
- Hiring as Exploration: intro ~1,300 words, 10 paragraphs.
