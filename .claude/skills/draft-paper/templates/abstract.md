# Abstract Template

**Target length:** 150–250 words. Single paragraph. No citations. No equations.
**Required:** At least one paper section must exist before drafting — verify content first.

## Required elements (in this order)

1. **Setup** (1–2 sentences): the phenomenon and the question.
2. **What we do** (1 sentence): study setting + identification source.
3. **Findings** (2–4 sentences): each headline finding with magnitude and units. Lead with the biggest.
4. **Mechanism / framework** (1 sentence, optional): what explains the finding.
5. **Implications / so-what** (1 sentence): what this means for the field or for policy.

## Sentence-by-sentence scaffold

### Sentence 1 — Hook
- The phenomenon, stated declaratively. No jargon. Avoid "This paper studies…"
- Example: "Algorithms increasingly mediate consumer-facing decisions in housing markets."

### Sentence 2 — Tension
- The question the paper engages. State it as a puzzle, not a gap.
- Example: "Whether and how algorithmic adoption shifts prices, allocations, and racial disparities remains unclear."

### Sentence 3 — What we do
- Setting + data + identification source, in one sentence.
- Example: "I study county-level digitization of property records, which creates plausibly exogenous variation in the entry of algorithmic investors into housing markets."

### Sentence 4 — Headline finding
- The biggest result with magnitude and units. Lead with the number.
- Example: "Algorithmic entry raises sale prices by 5% and shrinks the race penalty from 5.8% to 3.2%."

### Sentences 5–7 — Supporting findings
- 1–3 additional findings, each with magnitude. Same template.

### Sentence 8 (optional) — Mechanism
- One sentence on why the finding holds.
- Example: "Algorithms reduce a race-correlated noise term in pricing decisions, narrowing the human–algorithm gap."

### Sentence 9 — Implication
- The so-what. What does the paper change?
- Example: "These results show that algorithmic adoption has market-level distributional consequences that traditional firm-level studies miss."

## Banned patterns
- "This paper studies / examines / investigates X." → drop and lead with the phenomenon.
- "We find statistically significant effects." → state the magnitude.
- "Our results have important implications." → state what the implication is.
- "Future research should …" → no.

## Length norms from example papers
- Generative AI at Work: 235 words, 9 sentences.
- Market Effects of Algorithms: 220 words, 8 sentences.
- Hiring as Exploration: 195 words, 7 sentences.
